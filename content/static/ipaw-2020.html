Abstract

The replication crisis is now widely acknowledged across academic fields. Open Research practices such as publishing datasets, documenting computational methods and improving reproducibility with containers are gradually being taken up by some research communities. However, the additional data, metadata, provenance and software artifacts that such practices produce are often fragmented across multiple repositories and services. The notion of a Research Object has been proposed as a mechanism to gather these resources in a package or archive combined with structured machine-readable metadata.

To investigate the actual uptake of research objects and more broadly the usage of provenance metadata, we examined deposit patterns in Zenodo, a large and popular open repository of data, software and publications. After gathering and analyzing record-level metadata for the 3.5 million Zenodo records, we sampled and downloaded a subset of the 124k (~ 3.5%) deposited ZIP archives to further analyze their composition. Our study reveals that while most deposits lack embedded metadata or detailed provenance, there are beacons of hope, cases where we suggest depositors have been encouraged by best practices within specific communities and where metadata augmentation is inherently supported by their domain-specific tooling.

Based on this study and review of research object specifications, we propose recommendations for improving the practice of providing provenance and structured metadata in scholarly repositories.

Background

Reproducibility crisis as motivation

In the last two decades, academia has increasingly recognized the dangers of a reproducibility crisis [1] affecting published research across a number of diciplines [2]. While in general reproducibility can be considered achieving the same results using same methods, materials and data, the wider goal of replicability is finding equivalent results using the same methods but on new data.

Significant advances in scholarly practices have emerged, in particular journals now frequently require underlying data to be made availabilty in open repositories, and use of blanket statements like "data available on request" is starting to be recognized as bad practice.

While an increasing amount of scientific data is now digitally collected and subsequently made openly available, often the data is processed without sufficient statistical and analytical skills [3] or without taking simple robustness measures to ensure the software methods are understandable and reusable by other researchers [4] (this may include future self).

The practice of using scientific workflows to capture the digital analysis promoted early to improve reproducibility and consistency [6], but also to capture provenance of workflow executions [7] that track how result data is derived from other data. Many alternative workflow management systems have since evolved [8], covering a wide range of domains and computational models.

While early workflow systems could be seen as a way to formalize documentation of computational methods, as well as simplifying graphical access to complex computational resources (web services, clusters, grids), recent years have seen a growth in use of more script-like workflow systems that utillize cloud computing for scalability, containers and package management tools (Docker, Singularity, Conda) for reliably executing open source tools in their latest versions [8]. In wider field there has also been a reemergence of script languages for Data Science using interactive platforms such as R Studio and Jupyter Notebook, backed by rich visualization libraries. Open Research practicioners are now commonly sharing workflow scripts and notebooks in source repositories like GitHub and GitLab.

Software Citation practices [11], such as publishing code releases from GitHub with a DOI from Zenodo, are now actively encouraged by journals like GigaScience that encourage reproducibility. Funding councils are increasingly requiring Data Management Plans (DMP), where depositing in open repositores under a liberal license like Creative Commons Attribution (CC-BY) is highly encouraged. The FAIR principles FAIR that put importance on interoperability and metadata is being recognized by the European Commission [10], required by its next funding program and essential part of the European Open Science Cloud (EOSC).

It has however been argued that rather than of focusing on a narrow narrative of a "crisis", science should promote transparancy and reproducibility as a revolution that is empowering researchers to do better science [5]. While policy makers, developers and publishers have laid the foundation for Open Research and FAIR data sharing, a relevant question is to what extent researchers have already taken advantage of the new practices.
