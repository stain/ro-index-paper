Abstract

The replication crisis is now widely acknowledged across academic fields. Methods like Data Science and Cloud Computing is advancing to be applied by all domains, and Open Research practices like publishing datasets, documenting computational methods and improving reproducibility with containers, workflows and open source tools is gradually being taken up by eager researchers and enthusiastic communities.

However these additional data and software resources that support a traditional scholarly publication are often fragmented across multiple repositories and services, and add additional provenance and metadata challenges such as versioning, attribution, citation, access and persistent identifiers.

In this work we explore briefly well-established and emerging standards for structured metadata and provenance of scholarly digital objects and datasets, such as OAI-ORE, PROV, VoID, Research Objects, COMBINE archives, Frictionless Data, DataONE data packages, schema.org and DataCite Metadata Schema.

We then examine deposit patterns and the actual uptake of providing structured metadata in scholarly archives with the use case of Zenodo, a European-wide Open Repository of data, software and publications, popular across a wide range of research domains and hosting a variety of file formats and deposit types.

After gathering and analyzing record-level metadata across all of Zenodo, we sampled to explore deposits that contain ZIP archives, as we argue their composite nature of mixed content with potentially diverging provenance makes them prime candidates for utilizing manifests of structured metadata, augment record-level schemas such as DataCite.

Our study reveals that while most deposits lack such embedded metadata or detailed provenance, there are beacons of hope, where we find depositors are encouraged by best practices within specific communities and where metadata augmentation is inherently supported by their domain-specific tooling.

Finally we give recommendations for improving the practice of providing provenance and structured metadata in scholarly repositories, in particular with a view of supporting their automated consumption by downstream aggregators and individual researchers, aiming for a minimal provenance profile that can be largely populated automatically.
